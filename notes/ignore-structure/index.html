Structure is imaginary

Ben Miller

April 26, 2026

I’m experimenting with the dictation software to see what it’s like dictating into Google Docs. Getting access to Wispr Flow has been a godsend in terms of closing the gap between my thoughts and paper. Or rather my thoughts and a digital device or in AI.

This opens up my ability to express all of these thoughts and ideas that I’ve been meaning to express for quite a while. And I’m almost inclined to make this the primary focus of the next several months, at least for my part-time projects that I want to be working on.

I don’t know what form it’ll take at this point. There could be so many things that it could turn into. It could be a time set expressing some of my ideas related to urbanism and collective living. It could be an attempt to recount the random experiences and people and places that I’ve been in in an autobiographical way. It could be a wandering between all of those things and more in no specific order or structure. But hopefully getting at a few core themes or concepts that are difficult to approach directly.

And there’s a sense in which maybe I don’t have to worry about structure at all. In general, I don’t like the idea of having AI write for me or even edit what I come up with, but I might, might, be open to having it help to curate or identify pieces of the content that I produce that are worth highlighting or expounding upon. Or investing more time into it. If that’s the case, then I don’t have to worry so much about the structure of what comes out, because I can automatically identify the things worth going into or emphasizing. Not that I necessarily trust an AI to do it adequately. I don’t know the solution to that particular problem, but it is potentially freeing. I don’t have to worry so much about keeping a structure or editing things in a particular way.

And that’s the approach that, for example, Deleuze took in his writing about his rhizomatic philosophy. Where the work itself was meant to reflect the nature of what he was writing about, which is that, among many other things, previous to that, most philosophers would try to fit the world into an orderly, well-structured, tree-like shape.

And the point of what Deleuze wrote was to shatter that way of thinking and emphasize that things aren’t so orderly. Instead, you could maybe think about the world in terms of a rhizome, which nowadays in CS terms maybe we call it a graph. But the word rhizome sounds a lot cooler. I prefer having concrete metaphors to things. Especially when they’re botanical metaphors.

And acknowledging that lack of structure is very important, because that acknowledgement itself frees you up for finally moving and proceeding in a way that you never would have otherwise been able to. Because if you predicate on the assumption that there is a well-orderedness to your thinking or the information that you want to convey, if you start with that assumption, then that can be very limiting. Because then there’s a sense in which, before you proceed to broach whatever topic you want to get into, you feel that you have to structure things first. And so it’s important to acknowledge that there’s no well-structuredness to begin with. Once you acknowledge that, then you can wander into things more or less randomly. You can start talking about the most random thing, like the box of cereal sitting in front of you, or what you ate for lunch today, or what you’re emotionally concerned about at the moment. I don’t know.

There’s a sense in which you can start wandering into some random territory, and that very quickly you’ll touch upon some concept that you are wanting to get at. And beyond acknowledging that there’s a lack of structure inherent to your thinking, it’s also important to acknowledge that there’s a lack of even goal-orientedness. That there doesn’t need to be a goal in your writing, in your thinking, in your talking, in your doing. Once we acknowledge that, it’s all wandering anyway. That too, it’s an important thing to accept or embrace.

There is a sense in which this is also true of the software development process. People tend think that it can be done or should be done in an orderly, structured manner, where you have a roadmap that you set out and you have this series of features or epics or whatever that you work on. You take this series of steps, each moving towards some well-defined goal in the future. What I’m saying doesn’t just apply to the “waterfall” approach: it applies also to the more “flexible” methodologies, like Scrum or Agile. I don’t believe that they fully account for the natural chaotic-ness of software development. Or rather, the multi-directionality of it where at any point you can identify dozens of alternative paths that you might take in the development of that software.

Instead of it being a linear process or tree-like process, there is a very real sense in which, when you develop software, you tend to notice very frequently these potential features, or integrations, or improvements. Those grow according to the surface area of the software that you have. That’s what we’re seeing now too: an explosion in complexity. The more pieces that you have, the more surface area you have, the more directions you’re going to want to go in.

And so, there’s a very real sense in which now that we have access to powerful AI that can greatly accelerate the process of writing software, it could very well be that it completely explodes the surface area of the software that’s available to be worked on, and that is highly compelling to be worked on. I mean, I get how this Jevons paradox thing is potentially bullshit to try to make people less worried about the dangerous economic effects of AI. But I still think that there is a very real sense in which it could end up coming true, and resulting in there being a vastly larger domain of technical work to be done. Even if the technical work itself starts to feel far technical than it used to.
