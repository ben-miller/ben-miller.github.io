Sheetwork: Rationale

Ben Miller

April 24, 2026

I want to do a quick overview of what I’ve been working on. This project I’m calling it Sheetwork - for now, anyway. Maybe I’ll come up with a better name in the future but that’s sort of what stuck.

This project started as a set of Typst-based PDF file generators. Basically I wanted to create daily checklists for myself to help run my life. But not just checklists, physical checklists. Checklists that I can print out on a daily basis, typically the night before, allowing me to have a planning session where I sort of lay out what tomorrow is going to look like. It helps me to make a ritual out of the planning and a ritual out of the execution of the list the next day. This came out of the time when I had very little structure to my life and any little bit of structure had the capacity to go a long way in helping me to avoid chaos.

It strikes me as something potentially useful for somebody with ADHD or any kind of executive functioning disorder because it makes tangible this idea of accomplishing things and getting things done. And I think the problem with digital to-do applications is that it’s hard to maintain a sense of relative importance when you have an application that’s just sitting in a digital sea of other applications and the whole internet itself. It can be hard to naturally have that sense of salience about what’s important and what’s not.

People manage with this all the time but I think there’s something wrong when you need to sit there and open your phone or open your computer just to have an idea of what you’re trying to accomplish. And it’s not even so much that that’s the problem, I mean I guess, but I guess beyond that there’s a sense of accomplishment and there’s a kind of feel-good element to just being able to look at something physically. By the end of the day you have this checklist to look back on that, okay, I did everything that I set out to do and now at the end of the day I can feel accomplished. I can feel like my rest is deserved. And so yeah I mean, of course this is nothing new, this idea of paper-based systems, and there are some very good ones out there. I cannot recall exactly what they are at the moment but there are some genuinely impressive, very simple paper-based systems out there. I think they do have their niche and that niche is probably a lot bigger than the one I’m trying to fill with this application.

So I think, in general, I am very much in favor of paper-based systems. I think they just work with your brain a lot better. They feel better to use, they’re more satisfying to use, and they’re more psychologically powerful in a sense because they allow you to remember what’s important and what you’re supposed to be working on. In such a way that it doesn’t again just drown in a sea of digital competition for your attention. So anyway I do like these other paper-based systems but I think they are lacking in some sense. I do like minimalism but I think often minimalism comes at the cost of being too minimalist. I think very often some complexity can be necessary. In the case of this the complexity that I wanted to introduce was being able to have: programmatic routines, routines that change by the day, routines that are configurable, etc.

Because not every day is the same. Different days of the week are different. Some days are holidays, some days are work days, some days are just plain chaotic, some days are slow and deliberate. I think different checklists, different routines make sense based on the day. I think that most paper-based systems completely neglect that. And even beyond that I think that most paper-based systems can’t even do really basic things like help you to maintain a single routine day by day. Or at least in such a way that the routine can be adjusted fairly easily. So maybe for example you might have a monthly planner or a grid that you fill out once and then you try to fill it out. You try to get all the ticks in for each day and then you have this overview that you can look at and say, “Oh I did all 23 of my checklist items every day for a whole month and I got 90% of them checked.” Which I think is like an okay approach but also it undermines your ability to iterate and to adjust. I think that can be very critical to be able to do that.

So anyway this is sort of a rough sketch of the philosophy going into it: the thinking behind it, the reason why it developed in the first place.

This project started out as a Typst project where I was just generating a few variants of a checklist. I just did that every day for months. Each day I would plan the next and then, based on whatever my next day’s plan would be, I would adjust the checklist according to that. Along with these adjustments would come small code changes allowing me, in some sense, parameterizations that let me handle the various edge cases as they came up. It was necessary to make it so that I could repeat these adjustments in the future without having to re-write the code every time. And so that just sort of accumulated and over time you sort of hit these points where it made sense to redesign things and to take a step back and think about a data model and stuff like that. And so at some point this ended up growing into a weekly planner. Something that sort of roughly reflected what I was at one point doing in Trello, which was setting a weekday per column and just moving the cards between each weekday as it made sense to execute on a given task.

So anyway one of the things that I observed when creating these routines or these checklists on a daily basis was that you can think of a day, or planning out a day, in terms of there being various slots in which it makes sense to do something. And I found that planning based on these slots can actually be a very effective way of planning your day and managing your time. Because there are certain times during the day, certain locations, certain contexts that make the most sense to do a given task. And so if you can sort of anticipate what these are going to be in terms of the cycles and the routines and the stuff that is built into your life, then it can make planning things much more straightforward and powerful.

To offer an example, especially here living in Tokyo, you don’t have access to a car typically. Also shopping online, for instance the Amazon here, isn’t great. So an unusually high amount of effort goes into procuring the things you need to live, in other words, shopping and errands. And so beyond that also you have a limited amount of space in your backpack. You can’t just make one massive grocery run for the entire month all at once unless you have a car, which nobody has. And you don’t want to put it all in your backpack all at once. And so anyways it makes sense to have a bit of a system for it or rather to shop much more frequently so instead of making one big run for the entire month, you run the grocery store several times through the month.

Anyways this is kind of a silly example but it illustrates the point. When it comes to running to the grocery store, there is a very specific time that makes sense to do it and typically that’s on your way back from work, so like right after you get off the station that’s near your house. You can sort of design these schedules around time slots like that, saying, “Okay here’s my daily routine, here’s what it looks like,” and then there is this slot that makes sense for a very particular task. We’re going to make this slot that we can drag a task into.

So anyway that’s only one of the several core concepts that sheet work is designed around. But this is already getting to be very long so I’m gonna close this up. The point I wanted to make was that the system that I’m building is an attempt to integrate the benefits of paper-based systems with the power of digital planning systems. And I think nothing exists that does it.

Do I intend this to become a product? Probably not, although I do intend to polish this to a point where it might as well be a product. And not only polish it but make it scalable in such a way that if it were necessary to support thousands of users, it would be able to. So this is intended both as a serious learning project, a genuinely useful tool for myself, and an honest experiment in fusing digital and paper-based systems.
