Wasting time

2025/08/10

I seem to have become pre-occupied with the topic of gap years. It’s something that I have a history of doing, and a topic for which I hold a lot of passion and anxiety.

My latest gap has ballooned to nearly 3 years, in part because I’ve given up on getting back into tech (for now, anyway). When asked what IÂ have done with all this time, I don’t always know how to answer. I didn’t go into it with a plan, and looking back it involved a chaotic patchwork of short-term decisions, each making sense at the time. I’m still trying to make sense of what I did, what I gained, and what mistakes I might have made.

Some things I did: I learned to meditate. I read a lot. I saw some cool places. I met some cool people. I visited various friends and family members for weeks at a time. I explored my technical and mathematical interests. I grew my digital garden. I wrote stuff.

I learned to live without a rubric. Because there was nothing anchoring me, every morning I would wake up facing an infinite number of possibilities. It became an ongoing challenge to maintain focus. I learned to develop routines, making skillful use of notes, checklists, timers, and calendar apps. I learned to manage the most chaotic person I know: myself. You might envy being in a position of having this much freedom, but it can be overwhelming. Life is a lot simpler when you have someone there to impose some basic structure on your time. I had none such structure, but I learned to make do.

While I explored a lot, I accomplished little. I seriously cannot account for how I spent all this time. Lots of started projects that never came to anything. Lots of disruptions due to moving around every few weeks or months. Countless days spent too depressed to do anything.

In general, I wasted a lot of time. Maybe I regret it, maybe I don’t. There are certainly things that I would do differently. But I also recognize that chaos is a necessary side-effect of exploration. I don’t know how it’s going to pay off, or if it ever will. At least I am ripe with ideas, and if nothing else I have developed the profoundly worthwhile skill of meditation.

I don’t know if taking this approach to the extremes that I did was worth it. I wish I had more advice for someone wanting to do something similar. I really don’t know if I’d recommend it. It’s caused a lot of problems for me. It may turn out to have cost me my career. But if so, I’m glad to have taken one for the team. I wish more people had the courage to challenge unhealthy norms like employment defaultism.

It’s sad that we’ve cornered ourselves into a world where 2 days a week, and 2 weeks a year is all that we have to ourselves. That’s nowhere near enough time to explore your interests, master a skill, read the classics, learn a language, travel the world, or question your beliefs.

Wasting time is important, and more people could do it if they really wanted to.
