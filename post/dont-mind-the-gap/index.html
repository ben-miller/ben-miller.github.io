Don’t Mind the Gap

2025/07/30

I’ve taken two major years-long gaps over the course of my career: once from January 2016 to October 2017 (~2 years), and another from October 2022 to the present (~3 years). I realized recently that, out of the last 10 years, I’ve worked for only 5 of them.

On paper, this is not a great situation to be in. This is likely to haunt me for the rest of my career, as any astute interviewer will notice these gaps and have questions - if not serious concerns - about the time off.

Let me lay out some thoughts regarding this.

Firstly, I want to be completely forward about my attitudes toward work and career. I firmly believe in working to live, rather than living to work. I despise the term “career gap”, because it implies that a “career” is the default, with any time spent outside of it being an anomaly. Instead, I think of a period of employment as a “life gap”. The default mode of my life is doing whatever I want to do, and I view a “career” or “full-time job” is an interruption of that.

Still, I value my career and my craft, and I genuinely want to contribute, to work on stuff that matters, to be of value. I take what I do seriously, and I hold myself to a high standard of professional conduct. While I am concerned by certain elements of modern work culture, I understand and respect the implied contract between employer and employee.

But I also believe that this contract goes both ways. In particular, it involves boundaries, one of the most important of which is regarding how I spend my time outside work - whether it’s after hours or between periods of employment. I understand the need for an employer to conduct due diligence, but beyond that there is nothing more dystopian than the idea that my livelihood depends on me disclosing how I spend my personal time.

Given that, I refuse to sugar-coat my gap years. Despite the advice often given by recruiters, I won’t pretend that I was engaging in “freelancing” or “entrepreneurship” during this time. I’m not interested in truth-bending, or contributing to a kind of industry-wide equivalent to grade inflation. Sure, there were some small projects that I worked on, and some tech-related topics I explored. But to be completely frank, it has been years since I have been engaged full-time in a professional capacity.

Still, I believe that my time spent over these several years off has been overwhelmingly worth it, from both a personal and career standpoint.

Let me highlight a few reasons why.

Firstly, I believe that the benefits of extended travel are significant, but severely under-appreciated in American culture. People aren’t wrong in thinking that it can be used as an avoidant tactic, but in my experience most of the people I have met while abroad have been extremely well-adjusted, in no small part due to their extensive travel experiences. I reject the narrative that investing years of one’s life on the road makes a person shallow, avoidant, or unable to commit. On the contrary, it develops a deep sense of empathy, emotional intelligence, interpersonal skills, and cultural fluency.

  • You learn to deal with people. I’ve spent several years abroad, primarily staying in hostels and other socially-oriented spaces. Having traveled mostly alone during this time, I’ve been forced out of necessity to meet people and form groups. In a way, it’s been a years-long challenge of living outside of my comfort zone. It feels unaware to be making claims about this, but I will say that feel at-ease with most people, can carry on a conversation, and have acquired skill in navigating the nuances of interpersonal and group dynamics. Maybe this would have happened to some degree in a normal career, but nothing can match the variety of people and experiences that extended travel can expose you to.
  • You connect with people very quickly, many of whom you stay in contact for years. Essentially, most people you meet are in a special part of their lives, and are looking to branch out of their
  • You learn that there are a variety of life paths available to you.
  • You learn to research and plan. Every few weeks or months I am forced to undergo a new planning phase where I need to research visa rules, logistics, expected expenses, safety considerations, among many other things. It’s one thing if you have a lot of money to spend and can just book an expensive all-inclusive tour package. Quite another when you’re traveling long-term on a shoestring, trying to make every penny count.
  • You learn to manage information. Skillful travel requires a lot of nuanced information, much of it coming from conversation and direct observation. I’m always accumulating new pieces of information, and it’s so easy to let it get out of hand. Among other things, this has forced me to maintain a well-organized system of notes.
  • You learn to take risks and handle adversity.

Secondly, and in particular over these last few years, I was able to dive deeply into my meditation practice, taking it much further than most people would ever consider doing. I was able to spend literally thousands of hours practicing meditation, as well as studying meditation-related topics. I’ll post later about my thoughts and experiences with this. But for now, it suffices to say that depression and anxiety are no longer issues for me, and that life flows like it never has before.
